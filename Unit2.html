Unit 2: Standard Costing
Meaning of Standard Costing
Standard costing is a pivotal aspect of managerial accounting, oering a systematic method
to evaluate performance, control costs, and make informed decisions within an organization.
It is grounded in the establishment of predetermined costs, which are systematically
compared to actual costs incurred during production or other operational processes.
Standard costing provides a benchmark against which actual performance can be measured.
By comparing actual costs to the predetermined standards, management can pinpoint areas
of ineciency or excellence, facilitating proactive management actions to improve processes
and reduce costs.
Standard costing encompasses various elements of cost, including materials, labor, and
overheads. Each component is assigned a standard cost, reﬂecting the expected expenditure
under normal operating conditions. These standards are not static; they are subject to
periodic review and adjustment to align with changing business environments, technological
advancements, market dynamics, and other relevant factors.

In summary, standard costing is a cornerstone of modern managerial accounting, oering a
structured approach to cost management, performance evaluation, and decision-making in
organizations across diverse industries and sectors.
Cost Control: Standard costing enables organizations to set cost benchmarks, facilitating
the identiﬁcation of cost overruns and deviations from expected performance. This
insight empowers management to take corrective actions promptly, enhancing cost
control eorts.
Performance Evaluation: Comparing actual performance against predetermined
standards allows management to assess operational eciency and eectiveness.
Positive variances indicate superior performance, while negative variances signal
potential areas for improvement.
Decision-making Support: Standard costing provides management with reliable cost
data for decision-making. Whether evaluating pricing strategies, assessing the viability of
new projects, or optimizing resource allocation, standard costing furnishes the necessary
insights to make informed decisions.
Budgeting and Planning: Standard costing serves as a foundation for budgeting and
planning activities. By establishing predetermined costs, organizations can develop
realistic budgets and allocate resources eectively, aligning operational activities with
strategic objectives.
Types of Standards and Their Revision
Ideal Standards: Ideal standards represent the theoretical best-case scenario, assuming
perfect operating conditions with no ineciencies, wastages, or delays. While ideal standards
serve as an aspirational benchmark for performance, they may not be attainable in practical
settings. However, they provide valuable insights into the maximum potential eciency of a
process or operation.
Attainable Standards: Attainable standards are more pragmatic and realistic, reﬂecting
achievable performance levels under normal operating conditions. These standards account
for typical ineciencies, variations, and challenges encountered in day-to-day operations.
While attainable standards may not reach the level of ideal standards, they provide a more
accurate basis for performance evaluation and goal setting.
Current Standards: Current standards are based on prevailing operating conditions, taking
into account recent changes in technology, materials, processes, or market dynamics. These
standards reﬂect the best estimate of costs under current circumstances, providing a
practical benchmark for performance assessment. As operational conditions evolve, current
standards may require periodic review and adjustment to ensure relevance and accuracy.
Basic Standards: Basic standards are long-term benchmarks that remain unchanged for an
extended period, barring signiﬁcant shifts in the business environment or strategic
objectives. These standards provide stability and consistency in cost management eorts,
serving as a reliable reference point for performance evaluation and decision-making. While
basic standards oer continuity, they may necessitate periodic review to accommodate
substantial changes in external factors or internal processes.
